Warsh's emphasis on the Fed 'staying in its lane' and avoiding climate and social policy issues signals a more traditional monetary policy approach. However, his preference for alternative inflation metrics could prove problematic if energy-driven price increases persist, as trimmed measures might suggest lower inflation than what consumers actually experience. The transition period is occurring at a particularly delicate time, with the Fed needing to balance energy-shock inflation against potential economic softening from geopolitical uncertainties.

The combination of persistent labor market strength and rising energy-driven inflation creates a complex policy environment where traditional Phillips Curve relationships may not apply, as supply-side shocks can simultaneously drive up both unemployment and inflation.
