Unlike previous energy shocks that were primarily demand-driven, this supply-side disruption is proving more persistent and geographically concentrated, making policy responses more complex.

🇪🇺 European Central Bank's Measured Response

The European Central Bank demonstrated notable restraint this week in its response to the Iran war energy shock, with key officials emphasizing the importance of measured, strategic responses rather than reactive policy pivots. President Lagarde's keynote speech at the ECB Watchers Conference was particularly significant, acknowledging that 'we find ourselves yet again in a different world' but emphasizing that 'our response will be rooted in our monetary policy strategy.' Her framework outlined three key principles: assessing the nature, size, and persistence of shocks before policy decisions; recognizing that monetary policy cannot bring down energy prices directly; and identifying when higher energy costs become persistent inflation risks rather than temporary disruptions. Executive Board member Isabel Schnabel reinforced this cautious approach, arguing that the ECB 'shouldn't rush its reaction to Iran war' and should avoid overreacting to what may prove temporary supply disruptions. The ECB's February Consumer Expectations Survey, conducted largely before the war began, showed modest improvements in inflation expectations across time horizons, providing some baseline comfort. Vice President Luis de Guindos noted in his El Mundo interview that even in the most severe scenarios, the ECB doesn't expect recession, though growth impacts will be substantial. The ECB's approach reflects lessons learned from previous energy shocks, particularly the importance of distinguishing between temporary price level effects and persistent inflation processes.
