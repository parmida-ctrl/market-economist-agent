💼 Labor Markets & Fed Policy Recalibration

March's employment report delivered a decisive blow to dovish Fed expectations, with the 178,000 payroll gain representing the strongest hiring in nearly a year and far exceeding the anemic 59,000 consensus. This marked a dramatic reversal from February's revised -133,000 decline, though much of the strength was concentrated in healthcare (+76,000), with 35,000 jobs returning from the resolved Kaiser Permanente strike. Construction added 26,000 positions despite elevated borrowing costs, while transportation and warehousing contributed 21,000. The unemployment rate's decline to 4.3% was less encouraging, driven primarily by a sharp 469,000 reduction in the labor force rather than robust job creation. The three-month average payroll gain now sits at just 68,000, still reflecting the broader deceleration in hiring that has characterized the labor market since mid-2025. Initial jobless claims have remained elevated around 200,000-215,000, consistent with a cooling but not collapsing employment environment. Job openings data through February showed a further decline to 6.88 million from 7.24 million in January, maintaining the gradual normalization in labor demand. This employment resilience comes at a critical juncture as energy-driven inflation pressures mount, creating the exact stagflationary conditions that make Fed policy most challenging.

⛽ Energy Crisis & Inflation Dynamics

The Middle East conflict has now entered its fifth week with the Strait of Hormuz remaining effectively closed to commercial shipping, driving WTI crude prices above $104/barrel—a 49% surge for March alone and on track for the largest monthly gain on record. Brent crude has risen even more dramatically, up 55% month-to-date. This energy shock is propagating rapidly through the broader economy, with companies from shipping to retail already announcing price increases and surcharges. The U.S. Postal Service has proposed an 8% fuel surcharge on packages, while Chinese manufacturers are reporting 5-20% price increases on petrochemical-intensive products. Breakeven inflation expectations have responded predictably, with 5-year breakevens rising to 2.61% from 2.51% just two weeks ago, and 10-year breakevens climbing to 2.36%. Core PCE, the Fed's preferred measure, showed concerning acceleration to 0.37% monthly in January (annualized 4.4%), well above the Fed's 2% target. University of Michigan inflation expectations have shown volatility, declining to 3.4% in February from 4.0% in January, but surveys likely predate the most severe energy price increases. The key question is whether this represents a transitory supply shock or marks the beginning of a more persistent inflationary spiral, particularly given tight labor markets and elevated services inflation that preceded the energy crisis.

The Fed's balance sheet continues its modest expansion, reaching $6.675 trillion as of April 1, primarily driven by seasonal factors and ongoing quantitative tightening operations.
