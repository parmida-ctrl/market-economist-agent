⛽ Energy Markets and Stagflationary Pressures

The Iran conflict has created the most significant energy market disruption since the Russia-Ukraine war, with WTI crude oil reaching $114 per barrel - the highest level since 2022. More concerning for global supply chains, the conflict has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of global oil flows. UK traders warn that diesel stockpiles could be depleted by mid-May if the strait remains closed, while European jet fuel supplies face similar pressures. The energy shock extends beyond oil to natural gas and refined products, creating a broad-based commodity inflation impulse. This represents a classic stagflationary setup: rising input costs that simultaneously boost inflation while constraining economic growth. The timing is particularly problematic given that core inflation measures remain elevated - U.S. core CPI at 333.5 (up 0.72% month-over-month) and core PCE at 128.4 (up 0.47% monthly). Energy price increases are already showing up in financial markets, with 5-year breakeven inflation expectations rising to 2.56%, though 10-year breakevens remain more contained at 2.31%. The key question becomes whether this energy shock proves transitory or persists long enough to become embedded in broader inflation expectations and wage negotiations. Historical precedent suggests energy shocks typically last 6-18 months, but the geopolitical nature of this disruption makes duration highly uncertain.

This deceleration in European wage growth could provide some relief for ECB policymakers concerned about wage-price spirals. The key risk is whether energy-driven cost increases will lead to renewed wage demands, particularly in Europe where energy represents a larger share of household budgets. Historical experience suggests energy shocks typically lead to temporary spikes in unemployment as businesses adjust to higher input costs.
