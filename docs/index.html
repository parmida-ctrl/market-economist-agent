The University of Michigan survey showed one-year inflation expectations jumping a full percentage point to 4.8%, the highest since August 2025. This represents a dangerous unanchoring of expectations that could become self-fulfilling if it persists. Fed officials have repeatedly emphasized that expectations matter more than temporary price spikes, making this development potentially more consequential than the energy surge itself. The challenge now is whether the ceasefire can quickly reverse these expectation dynamics or whether the damage to credibility proves more durable.

The ceasefire announcement may trigger the latter, though the speed of recovery will depend on how quickly energy prices normalize and whether geopolitical tensions remain contained. However, the experience has reinforced the ongoing deglobalization trend, with companies likely to further diversify supply chains and governments to prioritize energy security over pure economic efficiency. The rapid market recovery following the ceasefire suggests confidence in existing supply chain adaptability, but businesses will likely accelerate efforts to build redundancy and reduce single-point-of-failure risks in critical supply chains.
