⚡ Energy Crisis & Geopolitical Shock

The Iran war has fundamentally altered the global energy landscape, with WTI crude oil surging from $83.71 on March 10 to peaks above $114 this week—a 36% increase in just over two weeks. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has created severe supply bottlenecks, particularly affecting refined products where stockpiles are considerably smaller than crude inventories. European diesel stockpiles are projected to be exhausted by mid-May if the strait remains closed, creating acute shortages for industrial production and transportation. The energy shock extends beyond oil to LPG and aviation fuels, with Asian air travel facing severe disruptions as jet fuel prices spike. UK traders warn that commercial diesel inventories could face critical shortages, potentially forcing industrial rationing. The spillover effects are cascading through global trade routes, with shipping costs rising sharply and supply chain disruptions reminiscent of the 2021-2022 period. This energy crisis differs from previous shocks in its breadth—affecting not just crude oil but the entire refined products complex, creating more persistent inflationary pressures across the economy.
